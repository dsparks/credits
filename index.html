Team-Building Credits Proposal Simulator

Team-Building Credits

Reforming the NBA draft lottery by realigning incentives and allocating talent to teams that need it.
Drew Cannon · David Sparks · Mike Zarren
Boston Celtics
The Problem

Goodhart’s Law and the Draft

“When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.”

For competitive balance reasons, and for fans to have hope, it is desirable to give teams in need of talent an advantage in acquiring good young prospects. Unfortunately, the way the league has historically identified teams in “need” of talent is to look at regular season losses, and this measure of need has become a target which teams can strategically pursue, to the detriment of the basketball product.

Ideally, we could find some measure of need that is not directly manipulable by teams. But nearly all proposed lottery reforms either fail to disentangle “need” from losing, or they fail to provide adequate relief for teams with true need. Counting wins after a certain point in the season creates an incentive for good teams to lose early, and doesn’t help teams that simply cannot win at any point. Restricting pick protections to narrow ranges just causes teams to pool toward those breakpoints. Considering record across multiple seasons creates an incentive to keep losing once you’ve started. Disallowing consecutive top-four picks makes it harder for talent to reach the teams that need it most.

Each of these proposals merely shifts the timing or breakpoint at which losing becomes the rational long-term strategy.

The Solution

Team-Building Credits

The Team-Building Credits system gives teams direct control over their relative advantage in acquiring talent, letting them determine need for themselves, and removing the incentive to falsely signal need by intentional losing. Credits cannot be manipulated through non-competitive strategies or lineups.

How It Works

Each year, the NBA allocates 100 Team-Building Credits to each team, minus deductions for playoff success. Credits may be saved or traded, but their primary use is to bid for draft position.

On a pre-specified date in late April, teams simultaneously submit sealed bids to the league. Bids must be integer amounts, and teams must have enough Credits to cover the bid. After the deadline, the league reveals all bids publicly.

Picks are allocated through a Rolling Window Lottery: the top four highest-bidding teams enter a lottery for the first pick, weighted by bid size. The winner is removed, the next-highest bidder enters, and the process repeats for each subsequent pick. The winner of each window is charged only an amount equal to the lowest bid in that window — so a team that bids high but falls to a later pick retains Credits for the following year.

Teams that bid zero are assigned picks at the end of the first round, with a random tiebreaker.

Second-round picks are allocated through a second stage of bids, which happens after the Rolling Window Lottery, in order of bid size.

Talent allocation

The NBA has historically prioritized giving non-contending teams the opportunity to build through the draft. Team-Building Credits preserve that opportunity. Teams can save Credits for the years in which they most acutely need draft talent, then invest heavily to maximize their chances. A rebuilding team can also trade veteran talent for additional Credits, further improving their draft position.

Trade market

Credits can be used as consideration in any trade, just like player contracts, portions of draft picks, and cash. A single Credit may serve as minimum consideration, replacing the classic heavily-protected second-round pick. Teams can also trade first- or second-round draft slots, or full future Credit allocations (subject to a Stepien-like rule), though these carry no protections or swap options, because the Credit bidding process makes those mechanisms irrelevant.

Teams holding multiple slots in a given year bid separately for each. Between the lottery and draft night, teams can trade specific picks to move up or back, as they do today.

Shaping Incentives

Offsets and Enforcement

Credit offsets for playoff success

The system need not allocate the same number of Credits to every team. Our proposal deducts 20 Credits for each playoff series won in the immediately preceding season. The NBA champion starts with 20 Credits; a team eliminated in the conference semifinals receives 80. This reflects the cyclical pattern of team success and gives teams outside contention better opportunities to improve, without creating an incentive to lose regular season games.

Credit penalties for rules enforcement

Because the league is the sole source of Credits, it can use this power for rules enforcement. Where monetary fines are currently used — player participation, injury reporting, tampering — replacing those fines with Credit deductions makes the penalties costly in basketball terms rather than financial ones. Docking a team a few future Credits is more granular and less heavy-handed than taking away future draft picks, which is one of the few recourses currently available.

Credits in support of league goals

The league could withhold or award Credits to support desirable behavior. For competitive balance reasons, there could be Credit deductions for teams spending over the tax, or for signing certain kinds of player contracts (although these would have to be bargained). Similarly, the league could award Credits to In-Season Tournament finalist teams, or to the teams of the players on the winning All-Star team.

Implementation

The Transition Period

The first Credit-bid draft would realistically take place in 2034 at the soonest. Once agreed to, all existing pick trades are honored, and teams continue trading picks under current rules through the 2033 draft.

In the first year after agreement, teams can trade 2034 picks and beyond as whole slots under the new rules. Teams can also trade their full allocation of Credits for a specific future year, up to seven years out.

Starting July 1, 2034, the league distributes Credits to all teams, and teams may no longer trade future Credits—only those they have accumulated. To smooth the transition to the new system, we recommend the league begins distributing smaller allocations of Credits to teams roughly three years before full implementation. These Credits could be traded immediately, or eventually used to bid on draft position.

Options

Pick Order Alternatives

The Team-Building Credits concept is the core idea; the mechanism for converting bids into picks is flexible. We propose the Rolling Window Lottery, but considered these alternatives:

Rolling Window Lottery — proposed

Teams ordered by bid. Picks allocated by smaller lotteries of 4 teams; winning team removed, next highest bidder added. Relatively low randomness. High bidders unlikely to fall far; low bidders can’t rise far. We do not believe a team would feel any significant incentive to lose a playoff series in order to receive 20 Credits.

Full Lottery

Each pick drawn from a lottery over all teams, weighted by bid. Highest randomness. Greatest potential for high bidders to fall and for low bidders to jump.

Deterministic Bid Order

Teams ordered by bid. Picks allocated in order of bid, with random tiebreakers. Almost no randomness. High bidders can’t fall; low bidders can’t rise. Potential for team-prospect collusion.

Top-N Lottery

Top several picks awarded by lottery, weighted by bid. All other teams’ picks awarded by bid size. Moderate randomness. High bidders unlikely to fall far; some small probability low bidders can jump up.

Questions & Answers

Q & A

What does the annual schedule look like?
  • July 1: the league allocates 100 Credits per team, minus playoff offsets.
  • Early February: the trade deadline applies to Credits and draft slots.
  • Mid-May: draft entry deadline and Credit bid submission.
  • Late May: the Rolling Window Lottery.
  • Late June: the draft.
How will the draft-pick penalty for teams in the Second Apron be implemented?

The first step of the penalty is easily carried over: we can simply “freeze” that team’s first round draft slot in the seventh following season, preventing them from trading it.

If a team is in the Second Apron for at least 2 of the next 4 cap-years, that team would then be prevented from bidding Credits toward that frozen pick, ensuring that it lands at the end of the first round.

This would be slightly less harsh than the current penalty since other teams could also bid 0 and thus this pick could be randomly drawn to be higher than 30 if there are other teams bidding zero. More importantly, the penalized team could still carry that year’s Credits over to a future year, so the penalty is less harsh than it is now, where all your “pick quality equity” for that year is taken away.
